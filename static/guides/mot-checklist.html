Pre-MOT Checklist: 15 Things to Check Before Your MOT Test

Around 40% of vehicles fail their MOT on the first attempt. Many of these failures are for simple issues you can check yourself. Use this checklist before your MOT to catch problems early and increase your chances of passing first time.

Lights and Signals

  1. Headlights: Check both dipped and main beam work. Look for cracks, chips, or condensation in the lens.
  2. Brake lights: Ask someone to press the brake pedal while you check all brake lights illuminate.
  3. Indicators: Test all four indicators and hazard lights. They should flash at the correct rate.
  4. Rear lights and number plate light: Ensure all rear lights work and the number plate is clearly illuminated.

Tyres and Wheels

  1. Tread depth: Legal minimum is 1.6mm across the central three-quarters. Use a 20p coin - if you can see the outer band, tyres may be illegal.
  2. Tyre condition: Check for cuts, bulges, or cracks in the sidewall. These are automatic failures.
  3. Tyre pressure: While not tested directly, incorrect pressure affects handling and wear patterns.

Visibility

  1. Windscreen: Check for chips or cracks. Damage larger than 10mm in the driver's view or 40mm elsewhere will fail.
  2. Wipers: Blades should clear the screen without smearing or juddering. Replace if worn.
  3. Washer fluid: Top up the washer bottle. The system must work during the test.
  4. Mirrors: All mirrors must be present, secure, and provide adequate rear view.

Under the Bonnet

  1. Oil level: Check and top up if needed. Low oil can cause engine warning lights.
  2. Brake fluid: Should be between min and max marks. If low, there may be a leak or worn pads.
  3. Horn: Must be loud enough to warn other road users.

Safety Equipment

  1. Seatbelts: Check all belts for damage, that they retract properly, and buckles click securely.

What Happens If You Fail?

If your car fails its MOT, don't panic. You have several options:

  • Get repairs done at the testing centre and request a partial retest (often cheaper)
  • Take it elsewhere for repairs and return within 10 working days for a free retest
  • If failure is for a "dangerous" defect, you cannot legally drive the car until it's fixed

The best approach is prevention. Use AutoSafe to identify your vehicle's weak points before booking your MOT, so you can address issues proactively.
